Discretisation Scheme Settings for OpenFOAM

Hrvoje Jasak gave a detailed presentation about the best practice guidelines for the Finite Volume (FV) discretisation in OpenFOAM at Portuguese FOAM User Group Meeting, 2015.1 Here we want to summarize the discretisation schemes in foam-extend 3.1 and present the best scheme in tet meshes.

Finite Volume Discretisation

  • Mesh quality is main concerns of FVM accuracy.
  • Mesh strcuture (hexahedral or tetrahedral meshes) determines the choice of appropriate gradient calculation algorithm.
  • For discretisation of convection and diffusion term, it is essential to use bounded differencing schemes.

Gradient Scheme

Gauss gradient scheme for hexahetral mesh

$$\begin{equation}
\int_{V_P}\nabla \phi dV = \oint_{\partial V_P} d \mathbf{s}\phi = \sum_f \mathbf{s_f}\phi_f
\end{equation} $$
$$
\phi_f = \frac{\overline{fN}}{\overline{PN}}\phi_P + (1-\frac{\overline{fN}}{\overline{PN}})\phi_N
\tag{2}
$$
Note:

  • If $\phi_f$ is face center value, the Gauss gradient scheme is second-order accurate.
  • This scheme is accurate on hexahetral meshes but losses accuracy to first-order on tetrahetral meshes due to large skewness error.

Least square scheme for tetrahetral mesh

Least square scheme does not use the face center value $\phi_f$. The starting point of the “least-squares” (LS) method for calculating the gradient of a quantity $\phi$ at the centroid of a cell P is the expression of the values of $\phi$ at the centroids of all neighbouring cells as Taylor series expansions about the centroid of P [3]. Then, for each neighbour $f$ it follows from the Taylor expansion that:
$$
\begin{equation}
\phi(\mathbf{N}_f) = \phi(\mathbf{N}_P) + \nabla(\mathbf{P})\cdot(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})
\end{equation}
\tag{3}
$$

A linear system $Ax=b$ with more equations than unknowns derived from Eq. (3) can be solved in the “least squares” sense. The best vector $z$ we can find is to minimise the error $b - Az$ when $b - Az$ is perpendicular to each column of A, i.e., $A^T(b-Az) = 0$. The gradient of a quantity $\phi$ can be shown as:
$$
\nabla(\mathbf{P}) = [(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})^T(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})]^{-1}(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})^T(\phi(\mathbf{N})-\phi(\mathbf{P}))
\tag{4}
$$
A weighting function $\omega = 1/(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})$ can be introduced to this scheme, which produces:
$$
\nabla(\mathbf{P}) = \mathbf{G}^{-1}(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})^T\mathbf{W}^T\mathbf{W}(\phi(\mathbf{N})-\phi(\mathbf{P}))
\tag{5}
$$
$$
\mathbf{G} = (\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})^T\mathbf{W}^T\mathbf{W}(\mathbf{N_f} - \mathbf{P})
\tag{6}
$$
The least square does not use the divergence theorem and is efficient and accurate to compute gradient of a quantity in tetrahedral meshes. Accounting for thin cells(e.g. boundary layer cells), a weighted least squares scheme is needed.
Note:

  • Least squares scheme is second-order accuracy on all meshes.
  • Cell- and Face-Limited Gradient is needed to bounded variables, especially when gradients are used for further discretisation.
  • Cell- and Face-Limited Gradient: Based on the gradient, calculate min and max neighbourhood value and apply gradient limiter to preserve bounds in cell centers.
  • Proposed setting for hexahedral meshes: Gauss or Gauss with limiters
  • Proposed setting for tetragedral meshes: least squares or least squares with limiters
  • I’ve seen it recommend to use LSQ unless you’re on a tet mesh because of osciillitatory behaviour. I’ve also seen it recommend to never apply limiters on p field and that p should use Gauss linear as it is not a convective term.

Convection Discretisation

Gauss theorem is applied to the convection term:
$$
\int_V \nabla\cdot(\phi\mathbf{u}) dV = \oint_S \phi(\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{u}) dS = \sum_f \phi_f(\mathbf{s}_f\cdot\mathbf{u}_f)
\tag{7}
$$
where $\phi_f$ is the face value of $\phi$ and $F = \mathbf{s}_f\cdot\mathbf{u}_f$ is the face flux: measure of the flow through the face.

Convection scheme for $\phi_f$

Simplest face interpolation is center differencing scheme, which is second-order accurate but causes osillations with a fine meshes. Taking into account the transportive of the convection term, we can using upwind differencing scheme with no oscillations but smearing the solutions.

  • Proposed Settings for hexahedral meshes: Starting with upwind scheme is a way to detect problems in current case. If upwind scheme doesn’t fail, a second-order linear upwind with optional gradient limiters is recommmended to apply. TVD/NVD schemes for bounded scalars (e.g. turbulence) with deferred correction formulation is recommended.
  • Proposed Setting for tetrahedral meshes: Upwinding scheme is recommended on simple cases. For highly accurate simulations, reconstructed schemes are needed.
  • ‘V’-schemes are specialised versions of schemes designed for vector fields. They differ from conventional schemes by calculating a single limiter which is applied to all components of the vectors, rather than calculating separate limiters for each component of the vector. The ‘V’-schemes’ single limiter is calculated based on the direction of most rapidly changing gradient, resulting in the strongest limiter being calculated which is most stable but arguably less accurate.

Face interpolation scheme for $\mathbf{u}_f$

A face interpolation scheme of evaluating $\phi_f$ from cell center values is essential to compute face flux.

  • The convection operator for bounded (scalar) properties must preserve boundedness.
  • A large number of schemes,e.g. TVD and NVD families, try to achieve good accuracy while preserving boundedness.
  • Special differencing schemes for strictly bounded scalars: switching to upwind differencing scheme when a variable violates the bound.

Diffusion Discretisation

Diffusion term is discretised using the Gauss theorem:
$$
\int_V \Gamma\nabla\cdot\nabla\phi dV =
\oint_S \Gamma(\mathbf{n}\cdot\nabla\phi) dS =
\sum_f \Gamma_f\mathbf{s}_f\cdot(\nabla\phi)_f
\tag{8}
$$
A scheme is needed to evaluate the face-normal gradient. For non-orthogonal meshed, a correction term may be necessary:
$$
\mathbf{s}_f\cdot(\nabla\phi)_f =
|\mathbf{s}_f|\frac{\phi_N - \phi_P}{|\mathbf{d}_f|}
+
\mathbf{k}_f\cdot(\nabla\phi)_f
\tag{9}
$$

The ““non-orthogonal correction” term depends on mesh quality: mesh non-orthogonality is measured from $\overline{PN}$ and $\mathbf{s}_f$. Limited non-orthogonal correction: explicit part clipped to be smaller than its implicit counterpart, base on the current solution
$$
\lambda\frac{|\mathbf{s}_f|}{|\mathbf{d}_f|}(\phi_N - \phi_P) > \mathbf{k}_f\cdot(\nabla\phi)_f
\tag{10}
$$
where $\lambda$ is the limited value.

  • Proposed Settings for Hexahedral Meshes: If max non-orthogonality is below $60$ degree, no special practice just Gauss linear corrected; If max non-orthogonality is above $70$ degree, non-orthogonality limiter: Gauss linear limited 0.5.
  • Proposed Settings for Tetrahedral Meshes: Always with non-orthogonality limiters. Control limiter based on boundedness messages on scalars

In all cases, monitor boundedness of scalars and adjust convection and diffusion schemes to remove bounding messages. In complex physics, consider physical properties of variables: boundedness and conservation.
